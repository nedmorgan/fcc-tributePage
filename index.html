A Timeline of Ansel Adams' life

  • 1916 - First visited Yosemite with his family. Ansel also received his first Kodak brownie camera during this trip.
  • 1918 - Adams contracted Spanish Flu and became very ill, but was able to recover.
  • 1927 - Adams produces hsi first portfolio in his new style, Parmelian Prints of the High Sierras.
  • 1928 - Married Virginia Best in the Best's studio in Yosemite Valley.
  • 1929 - Adams joins the Sierra Club and was hired as a summer caretaker at the Sierra Club visitor center in Yosemite Valley.
  • 1930 - Adams publishes his first book entitled Taos Pueblo. Mary Hunter Austin wrote the text of the book.
  • 1931 - 60 Prints of the High Sierras were exhibited at the Smithsonian Institute.
  • 1933 - Adams opens his first art gallery in San Francisco.
  • 1934 - Elected to the Sierra Club border of directors.
  • 1939 - Adams was named editor of U.S. Camera & Travel, which was the most popular photography publication at the time.
  • 1940 - Adams creates, A Pagent of Photography, which was the most important and largest photography exhibit to date.
  • 1946 - Adams is the recipient of a Guggenheim fellowship to photograph all of the U.S. National Parks.
  • 1952 - Adams became one of the founding members of the magazine, Aperature, which intended to be a journal of photography displaying its best practitioners and newest innovations.
  • 1955 - Adams began his annual workshops where he taught thousands of students.
  • 1963 - Ansel Adams and Nancy Newhall accepted a commission from Clark Kerr, the president of the University of California, to produce a series of photographs of the university's campuses to commemorate its centennial celebration.
  • 1971 - The Sierra Club established the Ansel Adams Award for Conservation Photography.
  • 1974 - Adams had a major exhibition at the Metropolitan Museum of Art.
  • 1981 - Adams was awarded the Hasselblad Award.
  • April 22, 1984 - Ansel Adams died of cardiovascular disease in Monterey, California.

At one with the power of the American landscape, and renowned for the patient skill and timeless beauty of his work, photographer Ansel Adams has been visionary in his efforts to preserve this country's wild and scenic areas, both in film and on Earth. Drawn to the beauty of nature's monuments, he is regarded by environmentalists as a monument himself, and by photographers as a national institution. It is through his foresight and fortitude that so much of America has been saved for future Americans.

- President Jimmy Carter, Presidential Medal of Freedom Remarks at the Presentation Ceremony, June 9, 1980
